Two Years of Financials
Rebuilt in One Month

A five-location restaurant group approaching a PE-backed growth raise had a problem: two years of financials built on a failed ERP implementation, filled with noise, errors, and missing data. No audit. No investor-ready reporting. No way to measure performance. And a capital raise that couldn't close without clean books.

The Situation

A Failed Implementation
at the Worst Possible Time.

The company had made the right decision: consolidate five locations from individual QuickBooks instances into a unified Restaurant365 ERP platform. A single source of financial truth across locations is exactly what a growing multi-unit operator needs — and exactly what institutional investors expect to see.

The implementation failed. Not catastrophically, but in the way that's harder to fix: data got in, but it got in wrong. Chart of accounts misconfigurations, inconsistent categorization across locations, missing transactions, and unmapped integrations meant the system was populated but not trustworthy. Two years of financial history had been rebuilt on a faulty foundation, and by the time the team recognized the problem, the errors had compounded across every report the business produced.

The books were technically maintained. They were not telling the truth. Nobody knew which location was performing, whether the group was profitable on an adjusted basis, or what the real cost structure looked like. And with a capital raise on the horizon — one that would require an independent audit of those same two years — the stakes of getting this wrong were existential to the deal.

Conditions at Engagement
  • 5 operating locations; 3 more in active development
  • Restaurant365 ERP in place but unreliable — 2 years of corrupted data
  • No reliable P&L at the location level or consolidated
  • Prime costs running higher than management realized — and uncaptured
  • Independent audit required to close pending growth capital raise
  • Month-end close process producing reports nobody trusted
  • Purchasing and inventory processes undocumented; execution inconsistent
  • No real-time visibility across locations, POS, labor, or events revenue
The Engagement

Three Phases. Six Months. Self-Sufficient.

The engagement was sequenced deliberately: fix the past before you can reliably measure the present, and fix the present before you can build operational discipline into the future. Each phase was a prerequisite for the next.

01
Forensic Rebuild
Under 30 days

Two Years of Financial Statements Rebuilt from Source Documents

The two-year financial history was reconstructed transaction by transaction against source documentation — bank statements, vendor invoices, POS exports, payroll records, and credit card statements. Every material transaction was traced to a primary source and re-entered with correct categorization against a rebuilt chart of accounts designed to match the actual business model.

This was not a cleanup. It was a forensic rebuild — the distinction matters. Cleanup assumes the structure is sound and the entries are wrong. A forensic rebuild starts from zero, with the source documents as the only authority, and reconstructs the financial record entirely. The result was a set of restated financial statements for each of the two prior years, with full supporting documentation, that could withstand independent audit scrutiny.

The independent CPA firm was engaged in parallel as the forensic rebuild progressed, enabling the historical audit to begin as soon as the rebuild was complete rather than sequentially.

02
Systems Rebuild
2 months

Restaurant365 Rebuilt. Processes Designed. Automation Installed.

The ERP configuration was rebuilt from the ground up: chart of accounts redesigned to match the business model, location-level reporting structure implemented, and all integrations — POS, payroll, labor management, and events booking — properly mapped and tested. The new configuration produced accurate, reconcilable data on day one of operation.

Purchasing and inventory SOPs were written for execution at the line level — designed to be followed correctly by line cooks and restaurant-level managers without requiring financial expertise. The SOPs were then layered with AI-powered automation that checks all inputs against expected ranges and flags discrepancies to senior management before they compound into reporting errors. The goal was to eliminate the human variability that caused the original implementation to fail.

A real-time KPI dashboard was built to pull data automatically from ERP, POS, labor management, and events platforms — giving management a consolidated, live view of performance across all five locations without manual assembly. The dashboard closed the loop between operational decisions and financial outcomes in a way the previous system never had.

  • Chart of accounts redesigned for the actual business model
  • All ERP integrations rebuilt and validated
  • Purchasing and inventory SOPs written for restaurant-level execution
  • AI automation layer to flag discrepancies before they compound
  • Real-time dashboard: ERP + POS + Labor + Events, consolidated
03
Operational Analysis
Parallel to Phase 2

The Books Told a Story Management Hadn't Heard Before

With reliable data for the first time, the true cost structure became visible — and it was different from what management had believed. Prime cost, the core profitability metric for restaurant operations, was running materially higher than reported. The issue wasn't operational incompetence; it was that the previous system had been unable to correctly capture and categorize costs, making performance measurement impossible.

The operational analysis identified three specific opportunity areas:

  • Menu costing: Recipe-level cost cards were rebuilt for every menu item, correctly pricing each consumable at current vendor pricing and yield-adjusted quantities. The prior menu costs had not been updated to reflect ingredient price changes or waste factors, creating a systematic understatement of food cost.
  • Labor modeling: Hourly sales data across all five locations was analyzed to build a forecast-driven staffing model. Overtime patterns were identified and eliminated through scheduling adjustments. Cross-training programs were implemented to run leaner staffs during non-peak hours without service degradation.
  • Vendor and purchasing consolidation: Purchasing was reviewed across locations to identify duplication, inconsistent pricing, and missed volume opportunities. Consolidated vendor relationships were established where appropriate.

What This Engagement Required

Restaurant finance transformations of this scope fail when they are treated as accounting projects. This was not an accounting project. The financial system was the symptom; the operational processes were the disease. Rebuilding the books without rebuilding purchasing, inventory, and labor management would have produced clean historical statements that immediately began accumulating new errors.

The speed mattered too. Moving the forensic rebuild, the audit process, and the systems rebuild into parallel workstreams — rather than sequential ones — compressed what would typically be a six-to-nine month timeline into a single quarter for the financial foundation. The operational improvements followed in the next quarter. The total six-month engagement delivered a self-sufficient, fully documented finance and operations function that the team could run correctly without outside support.

Following this engagement, the company retained Lautertun Partners to lead their capital raise — leveraging the financial infrastructure we had built as the foundation of the investment narrative.
